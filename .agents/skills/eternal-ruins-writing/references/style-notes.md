## Tone

- The book is atmospheric, but not purple. Sentences usually describe a place or rule plainly, then add one vivid image.
- The prose takes the setting seriously. It does not rely on wink-at-the-reader phrasing.
- Advice sections sound reassuring and practical rather than motivational.

## Direct Address

- Rules explanations frequently use `you`, especially when instructing the reader to choose, mark, roll, or decide.
- Setting and subsystem explanations often describe `the Wanderers` or `the Guide` in third person.
- A useful pattern is: third-person explanation of the subsystem, then second-person instruction for what to do with it.

## Formatting Patterns

- Display headings are visually uppercase in the book, but source Markdown in this repo can stay in title case if CSS handles presentation.
- Chapter prose generally starts with a short framing paragraph before moving into subheadings or procedure blocks.
- Lists are used when enumerating requirements, options, effects, or outcomes.
- Tables are used for rollable content and structured lookups.

## Diction

- Prefer `wanderer`, `guide`, `region`, `location`, `feature`, `detail`, `track`, `bell`, `oddment`, `fragment`, `Hope`, and `Despair`.
- Prefer verbs like `mark`, `clear`, `roll`, `choose`, `travel`, `make camp`, `arrive`, `encounter`, `determine`, and `describe`.
- Avoid filler such as `totally`, `a breeze`, `vibes`, or similar casual phrasing.

## Mechanical Prose Pattern

Use this sequence when drafting rules:

1. Name the subsystem or trigger.
2. Explain what it represents in the fiction.
3. State what the reader does.
4. State the result or interpretation.
5. Add an example or follow-up option only if it earns its space.

## Repo-Local Overrides

The repo root `AGENTS.md` overrides the source book where they differ:

- Use the Oxford comma.
- Avoid contractions.
- Never use em dashes.
- Avoid `X, not Y` contrast structures.
- Preserve heading hierarchy.
